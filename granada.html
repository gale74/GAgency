Grenada

Granada is a city and the capital of the Granada province in the Andalusia autonomous community of southern Spain. The city is situated along the Genil River on the northwestern slope of the Sierra Nevada, standing at an elevation of 2,260 feet (689 meters) above sea level. The Darro River enters the city from the east, flowing for approximately one mile before turning southward to join the Genil. The river has been canalized and covered along much of its course through the city.

The name Granada may have originated from the Spanish word "granada," meaning pomegranate, which is a fruit that appears on the city's coat of arms, or from its Moorish name, Karnattah (Gharnāṭah), possibly meaning "hill of strangers." Granada was first settled by the Iberians in the 5th century BCE and later by the Romans, who established the city of Illiberis. It was also the last stronghold of the Moors in Spain during the rule of the Moorish kingdom of Granada, which ended when it fell to the Catholic Monarchs Ferdinand II and Isabella I in January 1492.

Granada is a popular tourist destination, and it boasts numerous architectural and artistic monuments. The city is home to many churches, convents, monasteries, hospitals, palaces, and mansions built in the Renaissance, Baroque, and Neoclassical styles. The Gothic Cathedral of Santa María de la Encarnación, built from 1523 to 1703, is located at the center of the city and contains the Royal Chapel (Capilla Real), which houses the tomb of Ferdinand and Isabella and a display of heraldic decoration. The cathedral is richly decorated with jasper and colored marble, and its interior features many fine paintings and sculptures by Alonso Cano. The Carthusian monastery known as the Cartuja (1516) is situated in the north of the city. The University of Granada, founded in 1526 and housed in a former Jesuit college, is located near the restored monastery of San Jerónimo (1492). The Monument of the Agreements is adorned with statues of Christopher Columbus and Queen Isabella, created by Mariano Benlliure.

The oldest section of Granada, known as the Albaicín (Albayzin) quarter, is located in the northeast of the city. It features narrow cobbled streets and Moorish-style houses called cármenes. The Albaicín is bordered to the south by the Darro River, and on the other side of the river is the hill on which the famous Moorish palace the Alhambra stands, along with the Alcazaba fortress that guarded it and the Generalife, the summer palace of the Moorish sultans. The 16th-century palace of Emperor Charles V is also nearby. Other notable Moorish antiquities include the 13th-century villa known as the Cuarto Real de Santo Domingo and the Alcázar, which was built in the 14th century as a palace for Moorish queens. In 1984, the Alhambra and the Generalife were collectively designated as a UNESCO World Heritage site, and the Albaicín was added to the list in 1994.

The southern region of Granada hosts the city's administrative and commercial districts, while the western side is occupied by the contemporary residential areas. Granada boasts an active trade in agricultural produce and a diverse range of manufactures, such as liqueurs, soap, paper, coarse linen and woolen fabrics, metals, and machine parts. The city holds a prominent international festival of music and dance. Tragically, renowned Spanish poet and playwright, Federico García Lorca, was executed by a Nationalist firing squad near Granada during the Spanish Civil War's outbreak.
