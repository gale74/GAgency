Zaragoza

Zaragoza, also known as Saragossa, is a city and the capital of the Zaragoza province located in the central Aragon autonomous community of northeastern Spain. Positioned on the south bank of the Ebro River, it was initially a Celtiberian settlement called Salduba until the Romans conquered it towards the end of the 1st century BC. Augustus, the Roman emperor, turned it into a colony and named it Caesaraugusta. The town was one of the first in Spain to be Christianized and had a bishop by the mid-3rd century AD. In 380, a church synod at Zaragoza condemned the Priscillianist heresy, which involved absolute renunciation of all sense pleasures. The town was taken by the Moors in 714 and then taken by King Alfonso I of Aragon in 1118.

As the capital of Aragon, Zaragoza prospered for three and a half centuries. During the Peninsular War, the city was renowned for the heroic resistance of its citizens under Gen. José de Palafox y Melzi during a long siege (1808-1809) by the French, who ultimately took the city. María Augustín, the "Maid of Saragossa," was among the defenders, and her exploits are depicted in Lord Byron's poem "Childe Harold."

Zaragoza is home to two cathedrals, with the older one being the Cathedral of La Seo, or Cathedral of Salvador, built in a Gothic style (1119-1520) but with some remnants of the previous Romanesque church, constructed on the site of the first mosque in Spain. The Nuestra Señora del Pilar Cathedral is dedicated to the Virgin of the Pillar, the patron of Spain. The cathedral commemorates the alleged appearance of the Virgin Mary on Jan. 2, AD 40, standing on a pillar erected in honor of St. James the Great, whose shrine is in Santiago de Compostela. The cathedral was designed by Francisco Herrera the Younger (El Mozo) and commenced in 1681, with some frescoes by Francisco de Goya. The Gothic churches of San Pablo and the Magdalena from the 14th century and the Renaissance church of Santa Engracia are also notable.

La Lonja (The Exchange), a Plateresque Gothic building, the Palace of the Counts of Luna (1537), where the Court of Justice sits, and the 17th-century Palace of the Condes de Sástago y Argillo are all outstanding secular buildings. The Aljafería Palace, west of the city, includes an oratory dome and tower that are some of Spain's best examples of Islamic civil architecture. The University of Zaragoza was founded in 1474, but the buildings were constructed in later periods, with the medical school being its most well-known faculty.
