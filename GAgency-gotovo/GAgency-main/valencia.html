Valencia

Valencia is the capital of both the Valencia province and the autonomous community of Valencia in eastern Spain. It was the historical capital of the former kingdom of Valencia, located on the Mediterranean coast at the mouth of the Turia River in the region known as the Huerta de Valencia. The city's earliest mention was by the Roman historian Livy, who recorded that the consul Decimus Junius Brutus Callaicus settled the soldier veterans of the Lusitanian leader Viriathus there in 138 BCE. Valencia later became a prosperous Roman colony.

The Visigoths took over Valencia in 413 CE, and the Moors conquered the city in 714. In 1021, it became the seat of the newly established independent Moorish kingdom of Valencia, extending from Almería to the Ebro estuary. After a prolonged conflict, El Cid secured the city from the Moorish Almoravids, and it remained under his control until his death there in 1099. The Moors regained the city (and kingdom) in 1102. In 1238, James I of Aragon added Valencia to his dominions, but it remained a separate kingdom with its own laws and parliament until 1479, when it was united with Castile under Ferdinand and Isabella.

Valencia is known as the city of the 100 bell towers, and the most outstanding ones are the Gothic Miguelete Tower (1381-1424), adjacent to the cathedral, and the hexagonal Tower of Santa Catalina (1688-1705), a fine example of Valencian Baroque style. The city's most important church is the cathedral, La Seo, situated in the ancient city center. Valencia has many notable civic buildings, including the Lonja de la Seda (Silk Exchange), a splendid late Gothic (15th century) building, and the Palacio de la Diputación, which housed the parliament of the kingdom of Valencia. The Ayuntamiento (Town Hall), a modern building with important archives and the city historical museum, and the 18th-century Neoclassical Palacio de Justicia are other notable buildings.

Valencia has many botanical gardens, museums of art and ceramics, and educational institutions, including the University of Valencia (1499). The City of Arts and Sciences is a large complex that features a planetarium, science museum, and arboretum. Noted for its unique architecture, the complex includes L’Hemisfèric (The Eye of Wisdom), an eye-shaped building designed by Spanish architect Santiago Calatrava, and L’Oceanogràfic (Underwater City), Europe’s largest marine centre.

Valencia's renowned annual Fallas Festival commemorates St. Joseph, the patron saint of carpenters, and it draws thousands of spectators to the city each March. The festival features towering monuments made of papier-mâché and wax, called fallas or ninots, which are satirical or humorous in nature. On the eve of St. Joseph’s feast day, all the fallas are burned in the streets, except for those ninots that are voted the best, which are preserved in the city’s Museum of Las Fallas. The city also has a traditional bullfighting arena, and bullfights are a main attraction during the Fallas Festival.

Valencia's port, El Grao, exports agricultural produce such as rice, oranges, lemons, onions, and wine, as well as manufactured items including furniture, glazed tiles and ceramics, automobiles, textiles, and iron products. The city's other industries include shipbuilding and food processing, and services, including tourism, are also essential to the economy.
