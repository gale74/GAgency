Madrid

Madrid is a vast and modern city with a population of over four million and counting. The journey into the city may not be a picturesque one, with a stream of unremarkable suburbs and high-rise apartment blocks, but the city centre is a delightful surprise. It features narrow, atmospheric alleys and pockets of medieval buildings, interspersed with eighteenth-century Bourbon squares, and dotted with peculiar shops and bars.

In contrast to other historic cities in Spain such as Toledo, Salamanca, Seville, and Granada, Madrid has few architectural wonders. However, the monarchs acquired remarkable picture collections, which became the foundation of the world-renowned Prado museum. Madrid also boasts other impressive museums and palaces such as the Reina Sofía and the Thyssen-Bornemisza, which are state-of-the-art homes to fantastic arrays of modern Spanish painting, European, and American masters, including Picasso’s Guernica. This has made Madrid a top destination for art tours across Europe.

Besides the famous cultural attractions, the city offers a plethora of smaller museums, palaces, and beautiful parks and gardens, where sports fans can visit the Santiago Bernabéu, the home of one of the most successful football clubs in the world, Real Madrid.

However, the main attraction of Madrid is the lifestyle of the inhabitants, the madrileños. They hang out in traditional cafes, summer terraces, and pack the Sunday Rastro flea market lanes, or play hard and party very late in one of the thousand bars, clubs, discos, and tascas. The Madrid scene, famously depicted in Pedro Almodóvar's movies, remains the most lively and entertaining in the country, surpassing whatever Barcelona or San Sebastián might claim.

Madrid's city centre has improved significantly in recent years thanks to a series of urban rehabilitation programmes funded jointly by the European Union and local government. Improvements in the transport network, such as extensions to the metro and new ring roads, and excavation of road tunnels, aim to alleviate the congestion in Madrid's streets. The regeneration scheme for the area around the River Manzanares is particularly ambitious, aiming to make it a focal point for leisure and recreation. The downside of all this development is that the city still appears to spend much of its time with roadworks and civil-engineering projects.
